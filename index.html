Motivation and Objectives

Modern engineering and AI systems increasingly rely on large networks of interacting agents, from autonomous vehicles and robotic swarms to infrastructure and communication networks. Yet, coordinating these systems safely and efficiently at scale remains one of the hardest open problems in control and learning. As data-driven methods and foundation models pervade decision-making, the need for principled approaches that bridge learning, control, and safety has never been more pressing. This workshop confronts this challenge head-on. It brings together researchers from control theory, reinforcement learning, and generative AI to rethink how multi-agent systems can learn, adapt, and coordinate reliably. Topics include safety-critical reinforcement learning, scalable coordination under uncertainty, and the use of generative models as priors or planners for distributed decision-making, all aimed at pushing the boundary between theoretical guarantees and practical scalability.
